# Dungeon Crusade — Avalon Beckons

A six-volume dossier landed at the catalogue desk this morning bound in dark cloth, the spine stamped *Genesis of Evil*. The cover sheet bore a hand-written caveat in Daniel (@dungeondive)'s own ink: that he and the game's designer, one Roger Pearce, are friends — that they speak often, that they have plans — and that any reader should weigh his words accordingly. The Keeper, who has long held the view that an honest disclosure does more to clear the air than a hundred pages of impartial bluster, files the notice approvingly and reads on.

The series chronicles Daniel's first encounter with **Dungeon Crusade Book One: Genesis of Evil**, a five-years-in-development passion project of the sort the catalogue clerks have come to call *Kickstarter-shaped* — a singular vision, untrimmed by producers, brought to print by patience rather than ergonomics. The arc is uncommonly clean: a welcome to the land of Avalon, a storage walkthrough, two parts of spoiler-rich component archaeology, a small-table setup of the death-trap Tomb of Kaladar, and a verdict.

The frame Daniel lands on is that Dungeon Crusade is, in the lineage of Legends of Andor, a **layers-of-simple-systems** game — a patrol-route AI that gives the dungeon a life of its own, an upkeep-phase initiative that splinters six heroes into improvised sub-parties, a difficulty dial the *player* turns rather than the designer. And under all of it, the genuinely uphill battle of a 70-page conversational rulebook that buries simple rules in paragraphs of voice. The game beneath the book is, on Daniel's reading, very much worth the climb.
